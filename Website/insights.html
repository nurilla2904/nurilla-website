Uzbekistan overhauls technical regulation framework with presidential decree

19 February 2026

President Mirziyoyev signed decree No. 25 on 16 February 2026, mandating full alignment of Uzbekistan's technical regulation, standardisation, metrology and accreditation systems with international best practices. The reforms introduce market surveillance, a new national conformity mark, sector-by-sector adoption of international standards and significant institutional restructuring, with phased implementation running from 2026 to 2028.

Technical Regulation Standards Market Surveillance Conformity Assessment Metrology Accreditation Regulatory Reform

What has changed and why it matters

On 16 February 2026, President Shavkat Mirziyoyev signed a Decree No. 25, launching the most significant overhaul of Uzbekistan's technical regulation system in years. The decree aims to dismantle ineffective legacy control mechanisms and replace them with a risk-based, internationally aligned framework covering standardisation, metrology, conformity assessment and market surveillance.

Key institutional changes include:

  • The Uzbekistan Accreditation Centre is transferred from the Technical Regulation Agency to the Cabinet of Ministers, ensuring operational independence
  • The Uzbekistan Scientific Testing and Quality Control Centre is liquidated, with its certification bodies dissolved
  • The Uzbekistan Standards Institute is reorganised by absorbing parts of the former centre
  • From 1 July 2026, a new "Conformity Uzbekistan" (CU) national conformity mark replaces mandatory certification for products covered by technical regulations, assessed on a risk-based basis

Phased sector-by-sector implementation

The decree sets a clear timeline for adoption of full international standards across industry:

  • From 1 July 2026: Textiles, leather, furniture, electrical engineering, automotive and ICT
  • From 1 January 2027: Oil and gas, metallurgy, transport, construction products and medical devices
  • From 1 January 2028: Energy, chemicals, ecology and services

Technical regulations covering 17 product groups, including personal protective equipment, pressure equipment, medical devices, radio equipment and wheeled vehicles, are scheduled for review and replacement between 2026 and 2028, benchmarked against EU, Korean, Japanese, Australian and other leading regulatory frameworks.

The decree allocates 78.8 billion UZS (approx. USD 6.5 million) for adoption of international standards from 2026 to 2028 and 250 billion UZS (approx. USD 20.6 million) for the procurement of high-precision measurement standards and equipment in 2026 alone.

Business environment and compliance implications

From 1 April 2026, businesses in the conformity assessment sector benefit from:

  • Deductions from accreditation service revenues reduced 2.5-fold to 2%, retained fully by the Accreditation Centre
  • A prohibition on new state-run testing laboratories or certification bodies in sectors where two or more accredited private operators already exist
  • Permission to conduct conformity assessment against foreign standards required for export markets

From 1 September 2026, all test reports from accredited laboratories must be registered in the "e-akkreditatsiya" information system.

The decree also establishes a "Quality Assistant" digital platform and mandates that designated deputy heads of 36 state bodies and major state-owned enterprises act as Quality Managers, integrating international standards implementation as a measurable performance indicator.

What this means for your business

For international companies operating in or exporting to Uzbekistan, this decree represents a significant shift in the compliance landscape. The move to risk-based conformity assessment, the phased withdrawal of mandatory certification across key sectors and the introduction of the "Conformity Uzbekistan" mark will affect product approval pathways, testing requirements and market entry strategies. Companies in textiles, automotive, ICT, medical devices, construction and energy sectors should begin assessing how the phased adoption of international standards affects their supply chains, certification arrangements and regulatory timelines.

18 February 2026

President Mirziyoyev signed Decree No. 21 on 16 February 2026, formally closing the 2023 to 2025 phase of the national development strategy and launching a revised framework to 2030. The decree mandates 45 new sector and regional strategies to be completed by end of 2026, introduces a new digital performance tracking platform and makes ministers and regional governors personally accountable for results.

Investment Strategy Regulatory Digital Energy Trade

What has changed and why it matters

On 16 February 2026, President Shavkat Mirziyoyev signed Decree No. 21, launching a comprehensive reset of the Uzbekistan 2030 national development strategy. The decree responds to shifts in global supply chains, energy markets and technology, and sets out a new governance architecture to drive delivery across all government bodies.

Key governance changes include:

  • All ministries, agencies and regional administrations will operate against new measurable targets aligned to the updated strategy
  • A new Strategic Planning Digital Platform will track performance in real time using a traffic light rating system
  • The consolidated state budget must now be built around strategy targets
  • Ministers and regional governors are personally accountable for results

For international companies operating in or considering entry to Uzbekistan, this decree resets the strategic planning environment across virtually every sector. Companies in renewables, extractives, fintech, logistics, automotive and consumer sectors should begin identifying which strategies are directly relevant to their operations and whether early engagement with the lead ministries during the drafting phase is warranted. Once strategies are adopted, the scope for influencing their content narrows considerably.

Uzbekistan tightens mandatory digital product marking rules

26 January 2026

Cabinet of Ministers Resolution No. 23, signed on 23 January 2026, introduces a significant upgrade to Uzbekistan's mandatory digital marking system, setting a firm 1 July 2026 deadline for new compliance requirements across invoicing, point of sale systems and wholesale transactions.

Uzbekistan Remote Tax Audits Digital Marking Asl Belgisi

What has changed and why it matters

Cabinet of Ministers Resolution No. 23, adopted on 23 January 2026, delivers the most substantive upgrade to Uzbekistan's mandatory digital product marking framework in recent years. The resolution implements President Mirziyoyev's May 2025 directive on strengthening the Asl belgisi national product tracking system, tightening controls across invoicing, retail and wholesale practices and significantly expanding enforcement capacity.

Electronic invoicing and point of sale controls

From 1 July 2026, electronic invoices for mandatorily marked products will be subject to automated pre-clearance checks. The system will verify that the seller holds a valid licence, is registered in the Asl belgisi system and that the marking codes on the invoice are accurate. Invoices that fail these checks cannot be generated.

Parallel requirements apply to online cash register systems. When a marking code is scanned at point of sale, the system must automatically generate the correct product identifier data and attach it to the fiscal receipt. The system must also block any attempt to use a duplicate or previously used marking code, preventing counterfeit or recycled codes from producing valid sales receipts.

A further restriction applies at the wholesale level. From 1 July 2026, digitally marked products may only be sold wholesale via non-cash bank transfers. Cash-based wholesale transactions for marked products are prohibited from the same date.

New products entering the system

The resolution formally adds vegetable oil to the mandatory digital marking list under HS codes 1507 to 1515. The Tax Committee is authorised to launch a pilot marking project for vegetable oil immediately, with full mandatory marking entering into force from 1 July 2027. Medicines and medical devices, previously scheduled for completion by early 2025, are placed on a revised timeline with full mandatory marking now set for 1 July 2027.

A new dedicated enforcement body

The resolution establishes a dedicated Interregional Tax Inspectorate for Digital Marking Compliance within the Tax Committee, reporting directly to its chairman. The inspectorate is empowered to conduct remote tax audits of businesses where marking violations are detected, issue formal warnings, apply financial penalties and monitor the entire supply chain from import and production through to retail. It will also lead efforts to expand the mandatory marking product list.

Financial penalties collected through remote audits will be distributed with 80 percent going to the State Budget and 20 percent to the Tax Committee's special development fund. For large taxpayers, the split is 60 to 40.

What this means for your business

Any company importing, producing, distributing or retailing mandatorily marked products in Uzbekistan faces a material compliance deadline of 1 July 2026. Electronic invoicing platforms and cash register systems must be reconfigured before that date. Companies that have not yet registered in the Asl belgisi system, or that are operating with incomplete or informal marking practices, face elevated enforcement risk given the new inspectorate's remote audit powers.

Businesses in food, pharmaceutical, consumer goods, logistics and retail sectors should treat this as an immediate operational priority. We recommend auditing current marking and invoicing workflows against the new requirements without delay.

Uzbekistan introduces legal framework for artificial intelligence

23 January 2026

A new law establishing the first legal definition of artificial intelligence and setting general rules for the use of AI technologies has been adopted in Uzbekistan. Law No. 1115 amends the Law on Informatisation and the Code of Administrative Responsibility, introducing safeguards for fundamental rights and administrative penalties for unlawful processing of personal data using AI.

Artificial Intelligence Data Protection Personal Data Digital Regulation Administrative Liability Regulatory Reform

What has changed and why it matters

As artificial intelligence tools become increasingly embedded in business operations and public services across Central Asia, Uzbekistan has moved to establish a legal foundation for regulating the technology. Law No. 1115, signed on 21 January 2026 and adopted by the Legislative Chamber on 12 August 2025 and approved by the Senate on 1 November 2025, represents the country's first attempt to codify the concept of AI and establish ground rules for its use across government and society.

Key changes to the Law on Informatisation

  • Legal definition of AI. AI is defined as "a set of technological solutions that simulate human cognitive functions (including self-learning and problem-solving) and produce results comparable to human intellectual activity when performing specific tasks"
  • State policy objectives. The state is now explicitly tasked with creating the necessary conditions for the introduction and development of AI, including establishing legal, technological and economic foundations
  • New competencies for the authorised state body. This includes attracting investment in AI, building technical infrastructure for AI-based processing of government data and organising AI-related training and professional development
  • Safeguards for fundamental rights. AI-generated information resources and AI-powered information systems must not harm individuals' life, health, freedom, honour or dignity. Legally significant decisions affecting human rights and freedoms cannot be based exclusively on AI-generated conclusions

New administrative penalties

The law introduces a new offence under the Code of Administrative Responsibility. Unlawful processing of personal data using AI technologies, or their dissemination through mass media, telecommunications networks or the internet, carries a fine of 50 to 100 base calculation units (approx. USD 1,700 to USD 3,400), with confiscation of the instruments of the offence.

What this means for your business

For international companies operating in or deploying AI-powered tools in Uzbekistan, this law establishes important new boundaries. The prohibition on relying exclusively on AI for legally significant decisions will affect automated decision-making processes in areas such as HR, credit scoring, insurance and public services. The new administrative penalties for unlawful AI-based personal data processing add a compliance layer that businesses using AI for customer analytics, profiling or targeted services will need to assess. Companies should review their AI governance frameworks and data processing practices to ensure alignment with the new requirements.

Uzbekistan expands support for light industry integration into global supply chains

15 January 2026

A new presidential decree sets ambitious targets for Uzbekistan's textile, garment and leather industries for 2026 to 2027, including USD 4 billion in exports, USD 2.2 billion in foreign investment and 80,000 new jobs. The decree introduces financial restructuring measures for struggling enterprises, new tax incentives, overseas trade representation and a programme to establish partnerships with 40 international brands and sourcing companies.

Light Industry Textiles Leather Export Foreign Investment Tax Incentives Supply Chains Trade Fairs

What has changed and why it matters

Uzbekistan's light industry sector, particularly textiles, garments and leather goods, has been a central pillar of the country's export diversification strategy for several years. Presidential Decree No. 4, signed on 12 January 2026, represents the most comprehensive package of support measures to date, combining production targets, financial restructuring tools, export promotion infrastructure and tax incentives into a single framework covering 2026 to 2027.

Key production and export targets

The decree establishes the following targets for the textile and garment sector:

  • Production output to grow 9% in 2026 (to UZS 146 trillion, approx. USD 12.07 billion) and 10% in 2027 (to UZS 167 trillion, approx. USD 13.8 billion)
  • Foreign investment of USD 2.2 billion in 2026 and USD 2.5 billion in 2027
  • Exports of USD 4 billion in 2026 (including USD 700 million via e-commerce platforms) and USD 4.5 billion in 2027 (including USD 1 billion via e-commerce)
  • 80,000 new jobs across 2026 to 2027
  • Partnerships with 40 international brands and sourcing companies

Financial restructuring and enterprise support

The decree introduces a traffic light classification system for textile enterprises based on economic and financial indicators (green, yellow and red categories), with targeted support for each. Measures include:

  • Credits of up to USD 2 million per enterprise secured against purchased or self-produced raw cotton
  • Extension of existing commercial loan terms up to 7 years for enterprises assessed as recoverable
  • Refinancing of existing loans where interest rates exceed 7% in foreign currency or 16% in national currency
  • Rescheduling of January and February loan repayments for cotton procurement credits, with amounts distributed across the final six months of the loan term

Tax incentives and customs exemptions

From 1 January 2026 to 1 January 2028, cotton ginning facilities benefit from a 50% reduction in property and land tax rates. From 1 January 2026 to 1 January 2029, the decree provides:

  • VAT exemption on the sale of textile and garment enterprise assets held on commercial bank balance sheets to investors
  • 50% property tax reduction for one year for investors who acquire such assets and restore production within six months
  • Extension of existing profit tax, property tax, land tax, turnover tax and customs duty exemptions for the leather, footwear and fur sectors

Overseas representation and export promotion

The decree establishes trade offices and representatives of the Light Industry Development Agency in the US, China, Russia, Turkey, Germany, Poland, the UAE and Saudi Arabia, funded through the Light Industry Support Fund. Their core mandate covers attracting foreign investors, finding new buyers for export products, building partnerships with major sourcing companies and brands, and securing international grants and expertise for workforce development.

Uzbekistan's diplomatic missions abroad will also receive export promotion targets by country, with quarterly performance reviews.

International trade fair programme

The decree approves a list of 40 international trade fairs across 2026 where participation by local light industry enterprises will be supported, covering 25 textile events and 15 leather and footwear events across Europe, the US, Asia, the Middle East and Africa.

What this means for your business

For international brands, sourcing companies and investors in textiles, garments, leather and footwear, this decree opens a structured entry point into Uzbekistan's light industry sector. The explicit target of 40 new partnerships with foreign brands and sourcing companies, combined with overseas trade representation and trade fair participation, signals an active outreach effort by the government. Companies in these sectors should assess whether the financial incentives, customs exemptions and restructured enterprise assets present viable sourcing, investment or joint venture opportunities. The e-commerce export targets also suggest growing openness to digital trade channels.

Uzbekistan establishes creative economy regulatory framework and creative industry park

5 March 2026

Cabinet of Ministers Resolution No. 90, adopted on 3 March 2026, approves the regulatory framework for Uzbekistan's creative economy sector. The resolution establishes a Republican Council for Creative Economy Development, sets out the operating rules for a new Creative Industry Park with tax incentives for residents and defines 142 types of economic activity across 15 creative industry sectors.

Creative Economy Creative Industries Creative Park Tax Incentives Digital Economy Investment Regulatory Reform Startups Cultural Policy

What has changed and why it matters

Governments across emerging markets are increasingly recognising the creative economy as a driver of employment, exports and innovation. Uzbekistan has now taken a formal step in this direction with Cabinet of Ministers Resolution No. 90, signed on 3 March 2026. The resolution implements the Law on Creative Economy and Presidential Decree No. 64, putting in place three foundational documents that define the institutional, regulatory and operational framework for the sector.

Republican Council for Creative Economy Development

The resolution establishes a 15-member advisory council under the presidency, tasked with developing proposals for creative economy policy, identifying growth areas, attracting investment and promoting exports. The council brings together government officials, civil society representatives, leading entrepreneurs and international and local experts. The Culture and Art Development Foundation under the Presidential Administration serves as the working body. The council meets at least once per year and decisions are taken by simple majority.

Creative Industry Park

A Creative Industry Park is established on an extraterritorial basis across Uzbekistan, managed by a dedicated directorate structured as a limited liability company under the Culture and Art Development Foundation. The park offers residents access to co-working spaces, laboratories, virtual offices, consulting services, investor matchmaking and support for participation in international fairs and exhibitions.

Key features for residents include:

  • Tax incentives applied from the first day of the month following registration as a resident
  • The right to export goods and services via online platforms without customs cargo declarations
  • Virtual office facilities (except for VAT payers)
  • Access to venture financing for startup projects
  • Support with entering domestic and international markets

Membership fees are set at 0.1% of turnover for individual entrepreneurs and self-employed persons and 0.3% for legal entities.

To qualify, at least 80% of a resident's annual gross income must come from creative industry activities. Newly registered entities are exempt from this threshold in their first year, but incentives are recalculated and recovered if the threshold is not met by year end.

142 types of creative economic activity across 15 sectors

The resolution formally defines the scope of the creative economy through a list of 142 eligible activity types grouped into 15 sectors, including applied arts and crafts, architecture and urban planning, audiovisual arts, performing arts, fashion and design, publishing, digital technologies, advertising, visual arts and museum and gallery activities. The list covers both traditional crafts (such as ganch carving, atlas weaving, calligraphy and knife-making) and modern creative services (such as game development, motion design, SMM, copywriting and product design).

What this means for your business

For international companies in media, entertainment, digital content, fashion, design, advertising and technology, this resolution creates a structured incentive regime for establishing creative operations in Uzbekistan. The extraterritorial park model, combined with tax benefits and simplified export procedures for digital and creative products, may be relevant for companies looking to establish regional production or service delivery hubs. The broad definition of eligible activities, particularly in digital technologies, software development and advertising, means the framework extends well beyond traditional arts and crafts.

Uzbekistan introduces major pharmaceutical sector incentive and reform package

7 March 2026

Presidential Decree No. 35, signed on 5 March 2026, sets out a comprehensive package of measures to boost pharmaceutical production, attract investment, promote exports and strengthen R&D infrastructure. The decree establishes production targets of UZS 8.5 trillion (approx. USD 703 million), export targets of USD 300 million and investment pipeline targets of USD 1 billion for 2026, alongside tax exemptions, credit interest subsidies, technology transfer cost reimbursements and the creation of a new National Biopharmaceutical Research Institute and "BioPharma City" hub. Critically for international companies already operating in the market, the decree also extends mandatory digital labelling obligations to importers and redirects 75% of customs duties collected on imported medicines to a fund supporting domestic manufacturers.

Pharmaceuticals Biotechnology Tax Incentives Investment GMP Export R&D Healthcare BioPharma City Digital Labelling Importers

What has changed and why it matters

Uzbekistan's pharmaceutical sector has been the subject of sustained government attention over the past several years, with a series of presidential decrees progressively building out the regulatory, financial and institutional framework for local production and import substitution. Presidential Decree No. 35, signed on 5 March 2026, represents the most detailed package to date, combining production and export targets, financial support mechanisms, tax exemptions, institutional reform and workforce development into a single instrument. While the headline measures are designed to attract manufacturing investment, several provisions have direct compliance and operational implications for international companies already present in the market through distributors and importers.

Production, investment and export targets for 2026

The decree sets the following headline targets for 2026:

  • Production output of UZS 8.5 trillion (approx. USD 703 million)
  • Formation of a pipeline of investment projects worth USD 1 billion
  • Launch of USD 800 million in new investment projects
  • Exports of pharmaceutical products and services of USD 300 million
  • Mastery of production of 350 new products
  • Expansion of the Tashkent Pharma Park cluster into a regional biotechnology and pharmaceutical R&D and production hub branded "BioPharma City", with an additional 100 hectares of land to be allocated

Mandatory digital labelling — new obligations for importers

The decree amends Cabinet of Ministers Resolution No. 149 of 2 April 2022, which established the mandatory digital labelling system for medicines and medical devices. The key changes directly affect importers:

  • Importers are now included alongside manufacturers and registration certificate holders as entities entitled to receive labelling codes from the system operator ("Asl belgisi")
  • A new requirement is introduced: importers must hold a power of attorney from the foreign manufacturer, certified by the competent authorities of the relevant foreign country, confirming the right to obtain digital labelling codes for the products in question
  • Importers assume full operational responsibility for labelling imported products, including ordering and receiving codes from the operator, converting codes into digital identification markers and applying them to consumer packaging
  • A phased code inventory is scheduled: manufacturers by July 2026, wholesale distributors by September 2026 and retail pharmacies by November 2026

International companies selling into Uzbekistan via local distributors or importers should urgently review their distribution agreements to ensure that powers of attorney and labelling responsibilities are clearly allocated, and that importers are operationally prepared to meet the new timeline.

Redistribution of customs duties on imported medicines

The decree formalises a mechanism whereby 75% of customs duties collected on imported medicines are transferred monthly to the Pharmaceutical Sector Support and Development Fund. While this does not add a direct new charge on importers, it explicitly links import revenues to the funding of domestic manufacturing competitors, reinforcing the government's broader import substitution agenda.

Financial support for domestic manufacturers

From 1 June 2026, a range of financial incentives come into effect for local producers:

  • Reimbursement of 50% of technology transfer costs (capped at USD 50,000) or 50% of R&D costs (capped at USD 100,000) for manufacturers that begin producing generic equivalents within one year of the original drug's patent expiry
  • Reimbursement of 50% of the cost of obtaining EU GMP, US FDA GMP certification or WHO prequalification, including foreign consulting services, capped at USD 50,000 per enterprise
  • Reimbursement of 80% of registration, clinical trial and bioequivalence study costs (capped at USD 8,000) for manufacturers that bring into industrial production drugs or medical devices developed by domestic research institutes
  • Credit interest subsidies for high-tech pharmaceutical investment projects brought into operation within three years

Tax exemptions and incentives

Investment projects in pharmaceutical production, medicinal plant cultivation or processing launched before 2040 benefit from:

  • Land tax exemption for three years from the start of the investment project
  • Profit tax and property tax exemption for three years from the date the project is commissioned

These exemptions apply where revenue from pharmaceutical production or medicinal plant activities constitutes at least 60% of total income.

A reduced 5% profit tax rate applies until 1 January 2030 for non-resident royalty income from technology transfer and brand licensing used in the production of drugs and medical devices in Uzbekistan, unless international treaties provide otherwise.

From 1 April 2026, customs duty exemptions previously available to pharmaceutical producers are extended to manufacturers of biological active supplements (BAS) and cosmetics. This exemption does not apply to importers who do not carry out manufacturing activities in Uzbekistan.

GMP, BAS and quality standards

From 1 January 2027, enterprises holding a national GMP certificate will be permitted to produce biological active supplements on their certified production lines. From 1 January 2028, BAS products will require HACCP system implementation for licensing and will be subject to mandatory digital labelling.

Post-marketing supervision and distribution rules

The decree's roadmap provides for the development of new regulations on post-marketing supervision of pharmaceutical products (to be submitted to the Cabinet by August 2026) and on wholesale and retail distribution rules, product withdrawal and disposal procedures (to be submitted by September 2026). Distributors and importers should prepare for potentially tighter documentary and procedural requirements.

National Biopharmaceutical Research Institute

A new National Biopharmaceutical Research Institute is established by merging three existing bodies: the Uzbekistan Chemical-Pharmaceutical Research Institute, the Tashkent Vaccine and Serum Research Institute, and the Oriental Medicine Research Institute.

Pharmaceutical regulatory sandbox

The government is directed to introduce a regulatory sandbox for pharmaceuticals by September 2026, covering the testing and registration of drugs, medical devices, new products and other intellectual property outputs. This could potentially simplify market entry for new products.

What this means for your business

This decree operates on two levels. For companies considering manufacturing investment in Uzbekistan, it creates a structured and well-funded incentive regime combining tax exemptions, credit subsidies, technology transfer reimbursements and the expanded BioPharma City hub.

For international companies already operating in the market through importers and distributors, however, the decree introduces immediate compliance obligations that require attention. The extension of full digital labelling responsibility to importers, including the requirement for certified powers of attorney from foreign manufacturers, means that companies must review and potentially renegotiate distribution agreements well ahead of the July 2026 manufacturer deadline. The redistribution of import customs duties to the domestic manufacturing support fund, while not a new tax, signals a continued strategic push toward import substitution. Meanwhile, forthcoming changes to post-marketing supervision and distribution rules may impose additional procedural requirements on existing market participants.

Companies in pharmaceuticals, medical devices, BAS and cosmetics should assess the operational and contractual implications of these changes as a matter of priority.

Uzbekistan advances comprehensive new Law on Transport

3 February 2026

A draft Law on Transport, published for public discussion in January 2026, sets out a unified statutory framework consolidating all commercial transport activity across road, rail, air, water and multimodal modes under a single instrument. The draft introduces a mandatory registration and integration regime for digital aggregator platforms, an open electronic tender system for route licensing, new cabotage and transit corridor restrictions for foreign carriers, and a restructured multimodal liability framework. For international companies operating in or through Uzbekistan in ride-hailing, freight, logistics, automotive or consumer goods, the draft carries material compliance and contractual implications that require assessment well ahead of its expected adoption in Q4 2026.

What has changed and why it matters

Uzbekistan's transport sector has been subject to incremental regulatory development over recent years, but the draft Law on Transport represents a step-change in ambition. The draft is a single consolidated statute replacing fragmented sector-specific rules and introducing new obligations that extend well beyond traditional carriers to encompass digital platforms, aggregators and international operators. The draft was open for public discussion in January 2026 and is expected to be adopted in Q4 2026. Companies have a window to assess compliance gaps and where appropriate engage with the legislative process before the law is finalised.

Mandatory aggregator registration

Chapter 4 of the draft introduces a dedicated legal framework for digital platforms that match carriers with passengers or shippers, covering ride-hailing, freight matching and last-mile delivery aggregation. Obligations include incorporation as an Uzbek legal entity, a notification procedure with the Ministry of Transport, system integration with the Ministry of Transport and the National Agency for Prospective Projects, mandatory issuance of fiscal electronic receipts for all transactions, registration of all driver and courier labour contracts in the unified national labour register, and a restriction on onboarding carriers that do not hold a valid licence.

Companies operating aggregator platforms across the region through a non-Uzbek legal entity should review whether their current structure will satisfy the local incorporation requirement once the law is in force.

Open electronic tendering for routes

All urban, suburban, intercity and international passenger routes will be allocated through open electronic tenders administered by the relevant authorities. Any registered legal entity holding the requisite licence may participate. Routes deemed socially necessary but commercially unviable will be eligible for state subsidy. This replaces discretionary route allocation with a structured competitive process, creating both opportunity and displacement risk for existing operators.

Multimodal transport and unified liability

The draft defines a multimodal transport operator as a legal entity that assumes full legal responsibility for the entire journey across all modes of transport. A single multimodal transport document covers the entire journey and a single through-tariff applies regardless of the number of modes or carriers involved. New Civil Code provisions codify multimodal contracts and the liability of the organising operator. The multimodal operator bears liability to the cargo owner for loss, damage or delay across all legs including those subcontracted to other carriers.

Companies structuring cross-border supply chains through Uzbekistan should review their contract architecture with logistics partners in light of this consolidated liability model.

Cabotage and transit corridor restrictions

The draft introduces explicit prohibitions with direct implications for international road freight operators. Cabotage, meaning the carriage of goods between two points within Uzbekistan by a foreign carrier, is prohibited. Third-country transport by foreign road carriers, meaning the carriage of goods between two foreign countries through Uzbekistan, is also prohibited unless a bilateral or multilateral agreement provides otherwise. Transit corridors are designated by the Cabinet of Ministers and within approved corridors simplified customs procedures apply.

International logistics companies routing freight through Uzbekistan or using foreign subcontractors for domestic legs should review compliance with these restrictions.

Taxi and ride-hailing vehicle and operational standards

Non-fixed ride-hailing taxi services may be operated by registered legal entities or licensed self-employed individuals. Vehicle and operational requirements include a maximum vehicle age of 15 years, mandatory civil liability insurance, acceptance of aggregator-mediated or direct electronic payments, and display of a standardised taxi passport inside the vehicle.

What this means for your business

The draft Law on Transport operates across two dimensions. For companies already active in the transport and logistics market in Uzbekistan, it introduces specific new compliance obligations, particularly the aggregator registration and labour integration requirements, that may necessitate structural changes to operating models ahead of the expected adoption in Q4 2026.

For international companies using Uzbekistan as a transit or logistics corridor, the cabotage prohibition and transit corridor framework require immediate review of subcontractor arrangements and route structures. The multimodal liability provisions affect how cross-border logistics contracts are drafted and how risk is allocated between parties.

Companies in ride-hailing, freight and delivery aggregation, international road freight, automotive, retail, pharma and infrastructure should assess the operational, structural and contractual implications of the draft as a matter of priority.
